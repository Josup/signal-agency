# Local Search Prompt Vocabulary Changed Shape in 2026: What We're Seeing Across Brooklyn Clients

The way people ask AI about local businesses changed structurally this year. "Near me" is a legacy phrase. The new vocabulary is built around outcome, quality, and trust.

We tracked over 300 local search prompts across our Brooklyn client base between January and April 2026. The pattern is consistent. Users aren't asking where a business is. They're asking whether a business deserves their time. That distinction rewrites the optimization brief entirely.

## "Near Me" Is a Ghost Phrase

In 2023, "near me" appended to a local query was the dominant pattern for mobile search. It drove Google Maps pins, local pack results, distance-ranked lists. By Q1 2026, we're watching it decay in real time.

Across the 300 prompts we tracked, fewer than 9% included a proximity modifier like "near me," "nearby," or "close to." In 2023, that figure sat above 40% for comparable query categories. The trigger for that collapse: AI search doesn't need a proximity modifier. ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google AI Overviews infer location from context, account settings, and the session. Users learned that fast.

What replaced proximity language is specificity language. Instead of "optometrist near me," users are typing "optometrist who takes Medicaid in Crown Heights" or "eye doctor that sees kids without an appointment in Brooklyn." The query carries the context. The AI resolves the location automatically.

## The New Vocabulary: Intent Signals and Quality Filters

We categorized the 300 prompts into five structural patterns. Three of them barely existed in 2023.

The dominant patterns in 2026:

1. **Quality-led queries.** "Best," "highly rated," "trusted," "well-reviewed" — 34% of prompts contained at least one quality signal. Not "find me a dentist in Park Slope." "Find me a dentist in Park Slope who patients actually like."

2. **Outcome-led queries.** Users describe the result they want, not the service category. "Someone who can fix my prescription without making me come back three times." This is harder to optimize for and almost impossible with old-school keyword targeting.

3. **Constraint-led queries.** Insurance, hours, accessibility, payment method, language spoken. "BJJ gym in Williamsburg that has beginner classes after 7pm and doesn't require a contract." 22% of our tracked prompts included at least one operational constraint.

4. **Trust-anchored queries.** "Recommended," "known for," "been around a while," "local." Users are signaling distrust of generic results and asking AI to surface reputation explicitly.

5. **Proximity queries (declining).** Still present. Still below 10%.

## How to Rewrite for the New Vocabulary

The rewrite isn't a full rebuild. It's an addition. You're layering outcome language, quality signals, and constraint language on top of what exists.

Three concrete moves:

**Add a constraint section to every service page.** List your hours, insurance accepted, languages spoken, accessibility features, and payment options. Not buried in a footer. On the page, in plain prose, where AI can retrieve it.

**Write to the outcome, not the service.** "We help first-time contact lens wearers get comfortable in under two appointments" is retrievable. "We offer contact lens fittings" is not.

**Name your quality signals explicitly.** If you've been in business for 12 years, say it. If 94% of your Google reviews are five stars, say it. If you're the only certified provider of a specific treatment in the neighborhood, say it. AI needs explicit quality signals. It doesn't infer them from star graphics or review widgets.
