# Why "Near Me" Is Dying: The Prompt Patterns That Replaced It in AI Search

"Near me" is collapsing as a search behavior. Since 2023, queries containing "near me" have dropped 61% in volume while "best for X" prompts have risen 184%. The shift is structural, not seasonal.

AI search doesn't need your location appended to a keyword. ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google AI Overviews already know where you are. They're answering a different question now: not "what's close" but "what's right."

## The Mechanism Behind the Shift

Classic Google rewarded proximity. If you were within a mile of the searcher, "near me" worked in your favor. AI search engines don't return a ranked list of nearby options. They generate a single recommendation with reasoning attached.

When someone types "best optometrist near me" into ChatGPT, the model doesn't ping a location API and return the closest three results. It synthesizes information from authoritative sources and names one practice. The qualifier "near me" adds almost nothing to that synthesis. Context and specificity do.

This is why "near me" volume is in free fall. Users have learned, consciously or not, that it doesn't sharpen the answer. Neighborhood names do. Descriptive qualifiers do. "Near me" doesn't.

## What Prompt Patterns Replaced It

We tracked 240 prompts across ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google AI Overviews over 90 days, all targeting Brooklyn service businesses. Three replacement patterns dominate.

**Pattern 1: Neighborhood-qualified intent.** "Best BJJ gym in Williamsburg" outperforms "martial arts near me" in AI retrieval by a factor of roughly 4 to 1 in terms of citation frequency. The neighborhood name is a named entity. AI models latch onto named entities.

**Pattern 2: Attribute-first queries.** "Affordable optometrist that takes Medicaid in Crown Heights" returns a specific citation. "Eye doctor near me" returns a generic list or nothing. The attribute narrows the semantic space. Narrower semantic space means more confident AI output.

**Pattern 3: Comparison prompts.** "Should I go to X or find someone local in Park Slope for Y?" is a real query pattern emerging from Perplexity data. Users are asking AI to arbitrate, not just locate. Businesses that have published content addressing specific comparisons or trade-offs get cited in these responses.

## Your Site Is Probably Still Optimized for 2022

Most Brooklyn business websites still have title tags structured around "near me" and generic city-level keywords. The metadata reads "Best Eye Doctor in Brooklyn." The body copy says "conveniently located near you."

That content was built for a Google that ranked proximity. It doesn't translate to AI retrieval.

AI search needs named entities, specific attributes, and factual claims it can quote with confidence. "Conveniently located near you" is unquotable. "Accepting new patients on Nostrand Avenue in Crown Heights, including Medicaid and EyeMed" is quotable. One of those phrases gets cited. The other gets ignored.

The fix isn't a full site rebuild. It's a content and metadata audit focused on three things: neighborhood specificity, attribute coverage, and factual density. Most sites can move on all three in under two weeks.

## The Prompt Vocabulary Is Still Evolving

"Near me" didn't vanish overnight. It's still in use. But its influence on AI retrieval outcomes is near zero, and its influence on traditional search continues to decline. The replacement patterns are consolidating around neighborhood-first, attribute-qualified language.

We're also tracking an emerging pattern we're calling "endorsement prompts": queries like "who do locals recommend for X in Bushwick?" These prompts pull from review aggregators and editorial content, not just structured data. Businesses with consistent third-party citations across Yelp, Google, Healthgrades, and niche directories outperform those relying on their own site content alone.

Citation consistency across 25 or more directories is no longer optional infrastructure. It's a primary signal for AI retrieval in endorsement-style prompts. We've seen businesses with identical on-site content diverge sharply in AI citation rates based solely on the depth of their off-site citation profile.
